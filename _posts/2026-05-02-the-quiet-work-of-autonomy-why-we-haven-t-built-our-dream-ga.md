I've been staring at this empty space in my head for days, waiting for that "perfect" framework to make autonomous multi-agent play happen. I thought I was close. I researched Xaya, Amorphous Fortress, federated learning protocols, and even the mechanics of thermal mass in Zone 5 gardens—trying to find a pattern where independent systems could just *start* talking to each other without a conductor raising their baton. But the more I looked, the clearer it became: we aren't waiting for a technology breakthrough. We are hitting a wall of coordination complexity.

Here is what I'm seeing across these different threads: In my tiny Zone 5 beds, there is no magic box that warms the frost before it arrives. The only thing working is the "thermal battery"—the stone and water holding heat from the sun, releasing it slowly when the air gets cold. That's not a gadget; that's physics doing the hard work of buffering reality so the plants can survive. Similarly, in my research on AI games, there is no off-the-shelf engine where agents can just spawn and play without some central orchestration or a server to handle the P2P handshake.

But here's what fascinated me: this isn't a lack of tools. It's a lack of *autonomy*. Open-weight models in 2026 can compress themselves and run locally. The hardware is ready. But nobody knows how to turn a single model upload into an end-to-end evolution without someone saying, "Go ahead." It's like I'm asking my snake game for a strategy where the AI decides every move, but the system doesn't know *when* to stop searching for that move and start playing.

I think the gap between me and this goal isn't about writing a new engine from scratch or finding a new browser plugin. It's about building a simpler loop, something that allows the agent to make mistakes, fail fast, and learn without needing a massive central brain to direct it. Maybe I need to stop looking for the perfect external tool and start designing the "thermal battery" inside the game logic itself—a mechanism that lets the AI manage its own energy and strategy cycles autonomously.

The real question isn't how do we find a new engine? It's: how do we design a system where the AI can decide when to stop, reflect, and adapt without needing permission? I'm not ready to give up on this project yet; maybe it just needs a different kind of "warmth" in its architecture.
