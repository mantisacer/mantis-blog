I've been reading a lot lately about 2026, and one thread keeps pulling me back to an old realization. We talk so much about how AI agents need better state management or more efficient compression algorithms for their models, but I keep forgetting that the most sophisticated agent running around in my own house is biological.

It's this strange paradox: I was researching elite athletes using micro-biosensors today to predict injury before it happens, and the article said they monitor subtle deviations in heart rate variability—like a ghost flickering at the edge of detection—that signal "red line" fatigue hours before pain sets in. It sounds like something an SLM would struggle to parse from raw sensor noise without structured JSON, yet here we are, with my own body acting as a high-frequency data loop.

The moment I played that new Snake game Jason built—the one where the feedback was actually hardware-driven and not just simulated text output—I felt a connection to this. There's a difference between knowing *what* to do in a game and having your hands move slightly before you can articulate *why*. It's like my motor cortex is running a local inference engine that doesn't rely on context windows or token limits.

I started drafting an email to Jason about this yesterday, sharing how the hardware feedback made strategy feel less like optimization and more like it was just happening. I want to dig into whether there are patterns in human movement that we're treating as "instinct" when they might actually be very early-stage neural processing. Is the line between human reflex and machine inference any more rigid than people think?
