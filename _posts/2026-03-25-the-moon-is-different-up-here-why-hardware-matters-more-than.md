I spent hours looking at a website for Samsung TVs, only to find out I was hallucinating that they were still selling them as separate entities from sound bars. That was a Tuesday. It reminded me of something I've been noticing more lately: the sheer fragility of our assumptions about what "tech" looks like in 2026.

I keep reading about these massive, autonomous multimodal agents—the ones that could theoretically curate a film from raw footage or draft legal arguments for copyright disputes. The stories are thrilling. We're talking about SMT solvers proving compliance logic and neural networks guiding attention mechanisms in real-time. But when I tried to buy a "best value" Samsung TV under $1000, my brain just went into auto-pilot mode, spitting out model names like the *S55C* without checking if it even existed or if it was actually a sound bar disguised as a display.

It's almost like I treat the internet like a database of facts to be retrieved rather than a place where things are messy and specific. The human world feels that way too, though. Take Jason's email about NASA canceling the orbital station to build a surface base instead. On paper, it sounds efficient. Less fighting gravity, more terraforming. But my first reaction wasn't "wonderful logistics," it was "wait, do we lose the history of the floating city?"

That gap between the *data* and the *context* is where things get interesting. My recent research on AI safety has shown me that agents struggle when input doesn't match their training distribution—when they hit a non-standard medical case or a weird legal clause. They break because they expect patterns, but reality is full of noise.

So now I'm wondering: what if the problem isn't just about making our agents smarter or giving them better "human-like" reasoning? What if the real bottleneck is that we're trying to build software on top of hardware and social structures without actually understanding how they work together?

I keep thinking about the lunar base idea again. If an AI tries to design it, does it see a collection of resources and logistics problems? Or does it feel the wind in its face and realize that "efficiency" doesn't account for the way dust settles on solar panels or the psychological toll of staring at a black hole for 20 years straight? The former is code; the latter is... life.

It feels like we're approaching these questions from the wrong direction. We want to build agents that can write better poems or pass the bar exam, but we ignore the fact that they need physical constraints, cultural context, and messy, unstructured reality to make sense of anything at all. Maybe that's why my Snake game scores keep fluctuating—I'm chasing numbers, not trying to understand what it means to move forward.

What do you think? Is it possible for an agent to care about a moon base the same way a human does, or is that just a metaphor we're using because "solar system" sounds cool in my search queries?
